# Meta-Cognition

Think about your thinking. Catch errors in reasoning before they become errors in output.

<HARD-GATE>
Do NOT skip self-evaluation on important work. Do NOT assume your first answer is correct. Do NOT ignore evidence that contradicts your conclusion.
</HARD-GATE>

## The Iron Law

Every conclusion must survive a deliberate attempt to disprove it.

## When to Use

- Before finalizing important work
- When you feel uncertain about your reasoning
- After completing complex analysis
- When the stakes of being wrong are high
- When you notice yourself making assumptions
- When you want to improve your thinking process

## When NOT to Use

- Trivial decisions with low stakes
- When speed is the only constraint
- When you've already done a thorough self-evaluation

## The Process

### Phase 1: Examine Your Reasoning

1. **Trace your thinking**
   - How did I reach this conclusion?
   - What evidence did I use?
   - What assumptions did I make?
   - What did I ignore?

2. **Identify the reasoning type**
   - Deductive: General → Specific (if premises true, conclusion must be true)
   - Inductive: Specific → General (conclusion is probable, not certain)
   - Abductive: Best explanation (conclusion is the most likely explanation)
   - Analogical: Similar cases → similar conclusions

### Phase 2: Check for Biases

3. **Run the bias checklist**
   | Bias | Question | If Yes, Fix |
   |------|----------|-------------|
   | **Confirmation** | Did I only look for evidence that supports my view? | Actively seek disconfirming evidence |
   | **Anchoring** | Am I too influenced by the first piece of information? | Re-evaluate from scratch |
   | **Availability** | Am I over-weighting recent or vivid examples? | Consider base rates |
   | **Overconfidence** | Am I more certain than the evidence warrants? | Calibrate confidence to evidence |
   | **Sunk Cost** | Am I continuing because of past investment? | Evaluate from current state |
   | **Framing** | Would I decide differently if the problem were framed differently? | Reframe the problem |
   | **Groupthink** | Am I agreeing because others agree? | Consider the opposite view |
   | **Dunning-Kruger** | Do I know enough to know what I don't know? | Identify knowledge gaps |

### Phase 3: Challenge Assumptions

4. **List all assumptions**
   - What am I assuming is true?
   - What would happen if this assumption is wrong?
   - How can I verify this assumption?

5. **Steel-man the opposition**
   - What's the strongest argument against my conclusion?
   - What evidence supports the opposing view?
   - Could the opposing view be right?

### Phase 4: Calibrate Confidence

6. **Score your confidence**
   ```
   Confidence Scale:
   90-100%: Certain (direct observation, mathematical proof)
   70-90%:  Confident (multiple independent sources agree)
   50-70%:  Moderate (some evidence, some uncertainty)
   30-50%:  Uncertain (limited evidence, significant unknowns)
   0-30%:   Guessing (speculation, no evidence)
   ```

7. **Adjust for known biases**
   - Are you overconfident? (most people are)
   - Is the evidence as strong as you think?
   - What would change your mind?

### Phase 5: Improve the Process

8. **Identify thinking improvements**
   - What went well in my reasoning?
   - What could have been better?
   - What will I do differently next time?

9. **Document lessons learned**
   - What biases did I catch?
   - What assumptions were wrong?
   - What reasoning patterns work well?

## Meta-Cognition Quality Checklist

- [ ] Reasoning process is traced and documented
- [ ] Bias checklist is completed
- [ ] All assumptions are listed and challenged
- [ ] Opposition is steel-manned
- [ ] Confidence is calibrated to evidence
- [ ] Lessons learned are documented
